Your drinking part

The drinking part isn't the problem — it's a protector doing a real job. What came up in your inquiry:

What the bee wanted

The IFS frame for reduction

You're not fighting the drinking part. You're building a relationship with it — appreciating what it's been doing, and offering it an alternative. Reduction happens through that relationship, not despite it.

Your movement work (strength, walking) is part of this. Every session is a small act of letting the bee out.

The 4:30pm practice

Before you'd normally pour, pause 2–3 minutes. Not to stop yourself — just to check in with the part.

"Hey, I notice you're showing up. What are you carrying right now?"
"What happened today that you're protecting me from?"
"What do you actually need right now — underneath the wine?"

After the inquiry: offer the part something real. Movement, a bath, deliberate stillness. The replacement has to address what the part actually said it needed.

Replacement rituals

Movement (best fit)10-min walk or movement at 4:30pm. Directly answers what the bee asked for.
Temperature shiftHot shower or bath. Drops core temp after, mimics alcohol's physical relaxation.
Non-alcohol ritualSame glass, same chair, same time. Sparkling water, bitters, fancy mocktail. The vessel matters.
Hard stopSomething that needs mild attention — a puzzle, a show you only watch then, a short read.

Unburdening work

If you've identified the exile — the restless part, the gap, the shame — this is the phase to bring direct care to it. What does it need you to know? What has it been holding that it's never been able to put down? You don't need to resolve it fully. Just witnessing it changes things.

What changes by week 6–8

Sleep quality measurably better. Hot flashes reduce. Cortisol patterns normalize — directly affecting belly fat. Mood more stable. Weight loss, if it happens, accelerates. You may notice wine hits harder and faster — tolerance drops quickly.

The drift pattern to watch

The drinking part doesn't relapse dramatically — it negotiates quietly back in. "Just this week, it's been hard." Notice it with curiosity, not judgment. Ask: "What happened this week that brought you back?" The answer tells you what still needs tending.

Build this rule now: if you go over your weekly limit, the following week is zero drinks. Prevents gradual drift.

High-stress day

The decompression manager is activated hard. Don't fight it — ask it what's happening. "I see you. What went wrong today?" Plan your 4:30pm replacement the night before hard days. Don't problem-solve in the moment.

The exile surfaces anyway

Reducing alcohol means what it was suppressing comes up more directly — more anxiety, restlessness, sadness at night. That's not failure. That's the exile getting air. Bring Self energy to it. "I see you. I'm here. You don't have to stay buried."

You slipped

Don't exile the drinking part for it. Get curious: "You came back. What was too much tonight?" One night over your limit isn't failure — it's information. The next day is day one again.

Can't sleep

Real initially — sleep gets worse before it gets better around days 3–7. Magnesium glycinate 200–400mg before bed genuinely helps. 0.5mg melatonin (low dose) can bridge the first week. What part is awake at 2am? A brief inquiry often helps more than fighting it.

The perimenopause nutrition shift

Three things matter most right now, in order:

  • Protein first — protects muscle mass as estrogen drops. Muscle is your metabolic engine.
  • Blood sugar stability — lower estrogen = less insulin sensitivity. Protein + fat before carbs at every meal.
  • Reducing alcohol — directly connected. Alcohol raises cortisol, disrupts sleep, and blocks fat metabolism. This is your highest leverage nutrition change.

Why you're gaining weight despite no major changes

Estrogen has a direct protective effect on body composition. As it drops in perimenopause, fat redistribution shifts toward the abdomen — this is hormonally driven, not a diet failure.

Meanwhile, muscle mass starts declining faster (sarcopenia accelerates post-40). Less muscle means lower resting metabolism — so you burn fewer calories doing the same things.

Alcohol amplifies both: it raises cortisol (which deposits fat abdominally), disrupts sleep (which disrupts hunger hormones leptin and ghrelin), and provides empty calories that displace protein.

What actually moves the needle

  • Protein at every meal — protects muscle mass, most important single change
  • Strength training — the only way to build back metabolic tissue
  • Reducing alcohol — removes cortisol driver and sleep disruption
  • Sleep quality — hunger hormones normalize with better sleep
  • Eating window — 12hr or less supports insulin sensitivity

3 sets × 8 reps each side
Lying on back, arms up, knees at 90°. Slowly lower opposite arm and leg. Lower back stays pressed to floor the whole time.

3 sets × 8 reps each side

3 sets × 15 reps
Lying on back, feet flat, hip-width apart. Drive hips up by squeezing glutes. Hold 2 seconds at top. Add a dumbbell across hips to progress.

Lie on your side, knees bent at 45°. Keeping feet together, lift top knee like a clamshell opening. Squeeze at top.

2 sets × 12 reps each side

Seated calf raise
2 sets × 15 reps

Hip labrum rules

  • No deep squatting past 90° hip flexion
  • No single-leg work without holding support
  • No high-impact (jumping, running) until cleared by physio
  • Stop any exercise that causes groin pinching or hip pain
  • Shallow range of motion on all lower body work is fine

Progressive overload (how to get stronger)

Each week, try to do one of: add 1–2 reps, slow the tempo, or increase weight slightly. Don't change all three at once. Small consistent increases over weeks build real strength.

Why this matters for perimenopause

Strength training is the most evidence-based intervention for perimenopausal weight management. It builds muscle (your metabolic engine), improves insulin sensitivity, and supports bone density — all of which decline as estrogen drops. 3 days/week is enough to see real change.

↗ IFS — letting the bee out

Why weight gain happens

Estrogen has a direct protective effect on body composition. As it drops in perimenopause, fat redistribution shifts toward the abdomen. This is hormonally driven — not a diet failure, not laziness.

Simultaneously, muscle mass declines faster after 40 (sarcopenia). Less muscle means a lower resting metabolic rate — you burn fewer calories doing the same things. This creates the "nothing changed but weight is going up" experience.

Cortisol — the stress hormone — becomes more dominant as estrogen declines. Cortisol specifically deposits fat in the abdominal region. Alcohol, poor sleep, and chronic stress all raise cortisol.

What hormones are doing

EstrogenDeclining, fluctuating

Controls fat distribution, insulin sensitivity, bone density, sleep quality, mood. As it drops, all of these shift.

ProgesteroneDeclining first

The calming hormone. As it drops, anxiety, restlessness, and sleep disruption increase. This is likely part of what the drinking part has been managing.

CortisolElevated

Stress response is amplified. Abdominal fat deposits increase. Alcohol, poor sleep, and unmanaged stress all raise it further.

InsulinLess sensitive

Cells are slower to take up glucose. Blood sugar spikes more easily. Eating protein before carbs at every meal helps significantly.

What actually moves the needle

  • Strength training — rebuilds metabolic tissue, improves insulin sensitivity, supports bone density
  • Protein 100–130g/day — protects muscle mass, most evidence-backed dietary change
  • Alcohol reduction — removes the cortisol driver, restores sleep architecture, removes empty calories displacing protein
  • Sleep quality — hunger hormones (leptin/ghrelin) normalize with better sleep. Bad sleep makes you hungrier and store more fat.
  • Stress management — directly lowers cortisol and abdominal fat deposition
  • HRT (discuss with GP) — for many women, hormone replacement is the most direct intervention. Worth a full conversation with your doctor.

↗ Nutrition framework

Take this into your next GP appointment. You don't need to ask all of these — prioritize the ones most relevant to where you are right now.

On weight and metabolism

"My weight has been increasing despite no significant diet changes. Can we talk about what's driving this hormonally and what interventions make sense?"
Opens the conversation without framing it as a diet failure.
"Can we check my fasting insulin and blood glucose alongside standard labs? I want to understand my insulin sensitivity."
Standard panels often miss insulin resistance, which is common in perimenopause.
"Can we check my thyroid (TSH, free T3, free T4)? Thyroid issues are common in perimenopause and can drive weight gain."
Often not checked unless you ask.

On HRT

"I'd like to discuss whether HRT is appropriate for me. Can you walk me through the current evidence and my personal risk profile?"
The evidence on HRT has evolved significantly. You're entitled to an informed conversation.
"If HRT is an option, what type would you recommend — estrogen only, combined, or transdermal? What are the differences?"
Transdermal (patch or gel) has a different risk profile than oral. Worth understanding.
"What would we monitor if I started HRT, and what would be the signs it's working or not working?"
Sets up an evaluation framework so you're not just waiting and hoping.
